The USDA Cropland Data Layer has the most detailed published accuracy assessment. Producer's accuracy (correctly identified as this crop) shown for recent years.
Key finding: Crop type classification accuracy is strongly scale-dependent. National products like USDA CDL achieve 85–95% overall accuracy because they are dominated by a few major crops in large fields. Accuracy drops significantly for minor crops (<1% of area), smallholder systems (<0.5 ha fields), and cross-country transfer scenarios (15–30% accuracy drop).

Key finding: The most persistent confusions in crop type mapping are: (1) winter wheat ↔ winter barley — nearly identical spectral signatures in winter, only separable during heading/ripening; (2) corn ↔ sorghum — similar C4 temporal profiles; (3) grassland subtypes — temporary vs permanent grassland, pasture vs hay; (4) smallholder mixed cropping in sub-Saharan Africa where fields are below the Sentinel-2 pixel resolution.

Key finding: Satellite phenology accuracy improves dramatically with resolution: MODIS (500 m): 12–18 day RMSE → Copernicus HR-VPP (10 m): 7–12 days → HP-LSP/HLS (30 m, gap-free): 4–5 days. The improvement comes from eliminating mixed pixels and reducing temporal aliasing. For phenology, temporal density matters as much as spatial resolution.

ML Benchmark Results

Crop classification benchmark accuracy by method and dataset
Comparison of baseline models across benchmark datasets. Shows how accuracy varies with method complexity and geographic transfer.
Key finding: Geographic transfer is the biggest challenge in crop classification. Models trained and tested in the same region achieve 78–95% accuracy, but cross-region transfer drops accuracy by 15–30% (EuroCropsML, CropHarvest, France→Kenya). This is the domain adaptation problem — spectral signatures, crop calendars, and field sizes vary dramatically between regions.
